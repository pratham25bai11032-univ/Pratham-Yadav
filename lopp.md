## Introduction

This report has been prepared for the CSE0001 Digital Literacy portfolio. It is based on the repository README (`r4.md`), the course brief PDF available in the root directory, and the evidence files stored in the `Digital_literacy` folder. - To improve awareness of cybercrime risks and methods of prevention.

## Task-Wise Report

### Task 1: Digital Literacy Awareness Poster

The first task presents digital literacy in the form of a poster titled "Digital Literacy: Your Guide to Navigating the Online World." The poster explains digital literacy as the ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information using digital technologies. It also highlights useful digital tools for students and basic safe internet practices.

This task is effective because it simplifies a broad concept into a visually engaging format. The poster emphasizes three important safety habits: using strong passwords, thinking before clicking, and protecting privacy. These are core principles of digital literacy because they connect knowledge with responsible action.

From an academic perspective, the poster shows an understanding that digital literacy includes both productivity and online safety. It is not only about using apps or platforms, but also about making smart decisions while interacting online.

### Task 2: Student Digital Portfolio

The second task focuses on building a digital identity across professional and learning-oriented platforms. The portfolio includes screenshots of GitHub, Kaggle, and Stack Overflow profiles. These platforms are valuable because they help students present their skills, interests, and learning progress publicly.

The GitHub profile screenshot shows an introductory README with personal details, academic background, and learning goals. This helps establish a professional online presence and makes the profile useful beyond course submission. Kaggle and Stack Overflow further represent participation in technical communities centered on data, coding, problem solving, and peer learning.

This task demonstrates that digital literacy also involves online professionalism. A student's digital footprint increasingly matters for internships, collaboration, and career opportunities. Maintaining meaningful profiles on trusted platforms is therefore an important practical skill.

### Task 3: Coding and Collaboration Platform Engagement

The third task provides evidence of activity on HackerRank. The screenshot shows a successful coding challenge submission, confirming engagement with a platform used for technical practice and skill assessment.

This task is significant because coding platforms support self-learning, logical thinking, and digital confidence. They also introduce students to environments commonly used in technical interviews and skill benchmarking. By using such a platform, the student demonstrates the ability to work within structured digital systems, interpret outputs, and validate problem-solving performance.

In the wider context of digital literacy, this task reflects active rather than passive technology use. It shows participation in a digital ecosystem where problem solving, feedback, and improvement are all mediated through online tools.

### Task 4: Professional Email and Online Etiquette

The fourth task includes two PDF documents related to email etiquette and online conduct. These materials support one of the most important areas of digital literacy: communication. Knowing how to write professional emails and behave respectfully online is essential in academic, workplace, and collaborative settings.

Professional email etiquette helps students learn how to structure messages clearly, maintain a formal tone, and communicate respectfully with faculty, peers, and organizations. The do's and don'ts document extends this idea into general digital behaviour by promoting courtesy, responsibility, and awareness of public conduct online.

This task is valuable because digital communication leaves records and strongly shapes professional image. A student may have technical ability, but without proper communication habits, their digital presence may appear careless or unprofessional. This section of the portfolio therefore addresses the behavioural side of digital literacy.

### Task 5: Cybercrime Awareness

The fifth task is the strongest reflective component of the portfolio. It includes a case study on UPI and online payment fraud and a prevention checklist for college students in India. The case study explains how social engineering can trick users into authorizing fraudulent payments even when the technology itself is legitimate.

The example of UPI collect-request fraud is highly relevant because students regularly use payment apps for daily transactions. The write-up clearly explains a key safety rule: a user never needs to enter a UPI PIN to receive money. This single concept addresses one of the most common financial fraud patterns affecting young users.

The prevention checklist expands the discussion into practical digital safety habits. It covers strong passwords, two-factor authentication, public Wi-Fi caution, app permissions, phishing awareness, social media privacy, and cybercrime reporting channels such as `1930` and `cybercrime.gov.in`. This section shows that digital literacy includes awareness, prevention, and response.
